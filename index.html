Eagle Analysis

Target: ePlus (PLUS)

Strategic Overview & Focus Areas

Core drivers shifting ePlus from transactional reseller to embedded IT architect.

The company maintains a strict focus on providing trusted guidance, innovative and flexible technology solutions, and a full suite of differentiated services. Over the past five years, the fundamental shift driving profitability for the firm has been the transition to recurring "As-a-Service" consumption models.

Because subscription, SaaS, and consumption models are often recognized on a net basis, they consistently drive Gross Billings up at a disproportionate rate compared to Net Sales, while yielding significantly higher profit margins and deepening long-term customer relationships.

Divestiture of Financing

On June 30, 2025, the company executed a structural shift by selling its domestic financing business to PEAC Solutions. The $180M initial cash proceeds are being reinvested purely into high-growth tech and services operations.

Strategic M&A

Since 1997, nearly 30 acquisitions have been integrated to broaden product offerings, deepen engineering capabilities, and expand regional presence (e.g., Bailiwick, NSG, PEAK).

Human Capital Moat

Personnel are viewed as the primary differentiator. The firm actively recruits highly skilled talent holding thousands of technical certs across Cisco, AWS, Microsoft, and NVIDIA.

Supply Chain Navigation

Mitigating recurring volatility. The company previously wrestled with general semiconductor shortages; currently navigating an industry-wide shortage of memory/storage chips driven by AI demand causing rapid price hikes and delays.

High-Growth Technology Focus Areas

■ Artificial Intelligence (AI)

AI is a transformative force across industries. Demand for AI-ready products to enhance decision-making and automate tasks was a major catalyst for core product sales (Networking/Cloud) in FY26.

  • Launched "AI Ignite" program to help customers assess, enable, implement, and scale AI solutions.
  • Developed Secure GenAI Accelerator (hosted PoC environment for safe testing) and an AI Experience Center.
  • Distinguishes itself as the only U.S. partner with both NVIDIA DGX-Ready SuperPod and DGX-Ready MSP specializations.

■ Cybersecurity

Security grew every year from $476.3M (FY22) to $841.5M (FY26). Rising sophistication of cyber attacks and hybrid work environments make this an enduring necessity.

  • Leads with the "Compromise Nothing" program, facilitating holistic business resilience.
  • Provides comprehensive Managed Security Services, including Security Operations Center (SOC) and Managed Detection and Response (MDR).

■ Cloud and Data Modernization

A core foundation of the strategy is helping customers implement a multi-cloud strategy and guiding them through their "Journey to Modernization".

  • Accelerating cloud migrations and optimizing cloud costs.
  • Securing workloads across private, public, and hybrid environments.

Drivers of Changes by Major Category

1 Net Sales

Net sales experienced strong growth early in the 5-year period, dipped in FY2025, and surged to record highs by FY2026.

  • FY22 & FY23 Growth: Sales grew rapidly (13.5% in FY23) driven by broad-based customer demand for networking and security solutions to enable remote and hybrid workplace transformations.
  • FY24 & FY25 Shift in Revenue Recognition: While gross billings continued to rise across this period, net sales dropped to $2.0 billion in FY2025. This divergence was driven by a major industry shift toward Software-as-a-Service (SaaS), cloud computing, and subscription-based software. Because the company acts as an agent rather than a principal for these services, the revenues are recorded on a net basis (recognizing only the margin rather than the full transaction value), which artificially suppresses top-line net sales figures while improving gross margin percentages.
  • FY26 Resurgence: Net sales rebounded massively, increasing by $442.4 million year-over-year. This was fueled by spending from large enterprise customers investing heavily in networking, cloud, and security solutions, alongside a stabilization in the proportion of sales recognized on a net basis.

2 Gross Profit and Gross Margin

Gross profit climbed steadily, reaching $616.1 million by FY2026.

  • Product Margin Expansion: The shift toward "as-a-service" and subscription models mentioned above was the primary catalyst for gross margin percentage expansion. In FY2025, product gross margins expanded by 190 basis points specifically because of the higher proportion of third-party maintenance and SaaS subscriptions recognized on a net basis.
  • Services Margin Compression: Conversely, while the services segment grew rapidly in volume, its gross margins faced pressure in the latter years. Following the acquisition of Bailiwick in August 2024, professional services margins decreased because Bailiwick utilizes a much higher proportion of third-party contractors for service delivery, which yields lower margins than the firm's organic professional services.

3 Operating Expenses (SG&A, D&A)

Operating expenses grew every single year, escalating from $313.7 million in FY2022 to $449.9 million in FY2026.

  • Investments in Human Capital: The most significant driver of rising costs was headcount expansion to support growth. The firm aggressively hired engineering and customer-facing talent, increasing its employee base from 1,577 in FY2022 to over 2,150 by FY2025. The acquisition of Bailiwick alone added 441 employees in a single year.
  • Variable Compensation and IT Costs: Increases in gross profit directly triggered higher variable compensation and commission payouts for the sales force. Additionally, the firm incurred higher software, subscription, and IT maintenance fees to support its growing internal infrastructure, as well as a return to pre-pandemic levels of travel and entertainment expenses.
  • Acquisition Amortization: Depreciation and amortization steadily increased (reaching $26.5 million in FY2026) due to the amortization of intangible assets acquired from recent strategic purchases, including Network Solutions Group, PEAK Resources, and Bailiwick.

4 Operating Income and Net Earnings

  • The FY24 & FY25 Compression: Operating income and net earnings peaked in FY2023 but contracted over the following two years. This profit squeeze occurred because gross profit remained relatively flat during the transition to net-basis revenue recognition, while operating expenses (driven by inflation, wage increases, and the integration of new acquisitions) continued to climb sharply.
  • The FY26 Recovery: Operating income surged by 66.7% in FY2026 to reach $166.1 million. This recovery was driven by a massive $104 million increase in gross profit, which far outpaced the $36.7 million increase in operating expenses for the year. Furthermore, net earnings benefited from increased interest income resulting from higher cash balances held by the firm.
FY2026 Commentary: Robust expansion. Total Gross billings increased to $3.84 billion. Professional services sales grew 19.4%, primarily fueled by revenues gained from the Bailiwick acquisition. Managed services expanded 10.6% YoY driven by cloud services and enhanced maintenance support.

The Valuation Premium Shift

Over the past five years, the equity narrative for PLUS has shifted from a cyclical hardware distributor to a specialized services architect. This shift commands a higher price-to-earnings multiple as revenue visibility increases.

Margin Expansion via Services

The market applies a premium multiple to recurring service revenue over cyclical hardware sales. The firm's deliberate M&A strategy (e.g., Bailiwick in FY25 for $124.9M) directly expanded professional/managed service gross billings, driving equity valuation upward across FY25 and FY26.

AI Infrastructure Boom (FY26)

Positioning as the sole U.S. partner with dual NVIDIA DGX-Ready specializations allowed the firm to capture the enterprise rush for AI readiness, heavily influencing the massive 58.8% YoY net sales spike in the Telecom sector (driven heavily by Verizon).

Capital Unlocking (Financing Sale)

The June 2025 divestiture of the financing arm for $180M cash removed a capital-intensive segment, providing liquidity for pure-play IT expansion and share repurchases, interpreted by the market as a structurally bullish signal.

Supply Chain Volatility (FY22/FY23)

Historical dips in equity value (e.g., late FY22 and FY23) were driven by general semiconductor shortages disrupting physical hardware fulfillment. Recent pressures stem from AI-induced memory/storage shortages, resulting in rapid price hikes and delayed revenue recognition.

Federal SLED Contraction

Initiatives by the Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE) decreased available funds for State, Local, and Education (SLED) customers and forced early termination of government financing contracts, creating a top-line drag in public sectors.

Go-To-Market & Proprietary Tech

The company employs a highly consultative, solutions-driven go-to-market strategy targeting middle-market to large enterprises, and SLED institutions.

Consultative, Team-Based Selling

Account executives are trained broadly and supported by inside sales. To design custom solutions, they bring in pre-sales engineers and subject-matter experts to engage directly with customers. Selling based on business outcomes significantly increases cross-selling of bundled solutions.

Stakeholder Targeting: Technology procurement decisions are made outside traditional IT. Sales targets IT departments, lines of business, business units, and finance departments.

Sales Force Compensation: Mix of salary and commission. As representatives gain experience and build portfolios, commission becomes the primary component of their overall compensation.

Acquisition & Marketing Channels

  • Personal relationships and networking (Direct Sales).
  • Electronic commerce platforms (private marketplace experiences).
  • Direct marketing, digital campaigns, and social media.
  • Vendor partnerships: Joint marketing, targeted demand-generation. Vendors frequently provide financial reimbursements (MDF), outsourced services, and personnel to assist.

Contracting & Procurement

  • CMAs: Customer Master Agreements dictate terms/pricing for enterprise.
  • Standard Purchase Orders: Utilized for customers without formal CMAs.
  • Public Bidding: Frequent mechanism for SLED and government customers.
  • SOWs: Statements of Work govern service engagements (primarily fixed-price, some time-and-materials).

How Services Are Delivered

Consulting & Advisory

Gaining clarity and devising strategies that drive transformation (e.g., answering "We lack insight and guidance to streamline and modernize IT").

Managed Services

Offloading IT management to reduce complexity (e.g., answering "We spend too much time managing IT, instead of managing outcomes").

As-a-Service Consumption

Leveraging dynamic, flexible consumption models to scale quickly.

Design, Architect & Implement

Streamlining deployments, maximizing impact, and speeding ROI for complex initiatives.

Lifecycle Services

Realizing the full potential from IT investments from procurement to secure disposal.

Technology Resourcing

Expanding the customer's IT talent bench and meeting global service demands to solve IT skill gaps.

OneSource® Family of Software

A suite of cloud-hosted, proprietary software (Procure+®, Manage+®, Docpak®, Viewmark®). This is a foundational element designed to streamline how enterprises buy, track, and retire technology, granting deep integration into customer workflows.

Core Capabilities

  • Total Supply Chain Control Automates and optimizes procurement and management of owned, leased, and consumption-based assets.
  • Private Cloud Marketplaces Integrates directly with AWS Marketplace, Azure Marketplace, and Google Cloud Marketplace.
  • Front-End Procurement Automation Handles order aggregation, order automation, vendor performance measurement, reconciliation, and payment.
  • Lifecycle & End-of-Life Asset/change management, property tax filing, equipment audits, secure removal, and disposal.

Risk Matrix & Ownership Structure

1. Reliance on Key Customers & Vendors

Concentration Risk

  • Customer Concentration: Verizon accounted for 24% of FY26 net sales and 36% of Accounts Receivable. Loss or material reduction in spending severely impacts earnings.
  • Vendor Concentration: Cisco historically accounts for 32-44% of net sales. NetApp, HPE, Dell EMC account for another 23-25%. Altered partnership terms or direct-selling shifts harm margins.

2. Supply Chain Disruptions & Inflation

Operational Constraint

  • AI-Driven Shortages: Massive demand for AI workloads created an industry-wide shortage in enterprise computer storage and memory chips (rapid price hikes/delayed lead times).
  • Margin & Order Risk: Inability to pass price increases to customers (e.g., highly regulated Gov contracts) squeezes margins. Extended delays risk customer order cancellations while locked into supplier commitments.

3. Macro & Gov Spending (DOGE)

Market Exposure

  • Federal Spending Cuts: Dept. of Government Efficiency (DOGE) initiatives actively decreased SLED funding, caused early terminations of Gov financing contracts, and tightened public-sector lender pools.
  • General Economy: Inflation, high interest rates, tariffs, and geopolitical conflicts delay enterprise CapEx.

4. Structural & Operational Vulnerabilities

Execution Risk

  • Financing Sale: Removing a profitable segment makes the firm entirely reliant on core IT operations. Risk of not realizing contingent consideration tied to the sale.
  • M&A Integration: Acquiring ~30 companies carries severe cultural/technical integration risks and potential for hidden liabilities.
  • Talent Scarcity: Retaining engineers/AI specialists is vital; wage inflation compresses margins.
  • Credit Reliance: Heavy reliance on Wells Fargo credit facility ($500M floor planning / $200M revolving) to fund inventory.

5. Cybersecurity & Data Privacy

Liability Exposure

  • Growing Threats: As an MSP managing customer infrastructure/data centers, the firm is a prime target for ransomware, malware, and AI-enabled attacks.
  • Reputational Harm: A breach (internal or via third-party vendors) exposing sensitive data results in legal liability, regulatory fines, and lasting brand damage.

6. The Shift to AI and Cloud "As-a-Service"

Disintermediation Risk

  • Failure to Innovate: Rapid shift away from hardware toward SaaS/Cloud. Risk of losing market share to hyperscalers or competitors if skills/offerings don't adapt.
  • AI Ethical/Accuracy Risks: Integrating AI into operations/services creates new liabilities regarding deployment efficacy, AI errors, and social/ethical impacts.
